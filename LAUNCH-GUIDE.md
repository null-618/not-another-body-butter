# Not Another Body Butter — Launch Readiness Guide

*Everything required to go from prototype site to real customer purchases. Researched June 3, 2026 against primary sources (NC SOS, NCDOR, FDA, IRS, official pricing pages). North Carolina, LLC in progress, selling online + in-person at markets.*

## The Launch Checklist (in order)

**Phase 1 — Business foundation (1–2 weeks)**
1. ☐ File NC LLC Articles of Organization — $125, online at sosnc.gov (file directly; ignore third-party "priority filing" upsells)
2. ☐ Get free EIN at irs.gov (immediate, never pay a third party)
3. ☐ Open free business checking (Bluevine, Mercury, Novo, or Found — all $0/mo)
4. ☐ Register for NC Sales & Use Tax with NCDOR — free, instant account number online
5. ☐ Check your city/county home-occupation rules for making product at home (permit tier varies by municipality)

**Phase 2 — Compliance & protection (2–4 weeks, parallel with Phase 3)**
6. ☐ *(Optional — required if doing markets/pop-ups)* Buy general + product liability insurance (~$515–$645/yr all-in; see Insurance)
7. ☐ Bring labels into FDA/FPLA + MoCRA compliance (see Labeling — this is the most detailed compliance item)
8. ☐ Create a safety substantiation file per product
9. ☐ Tell your homeowners insurer about the home business
10. ☐ (Optional, recommended) File USPTO trademark, Class 3 — $350 one-time

**Phase 3 — Site & commerce (2–4 weeks)**
11. ☐ Buy custom domain (~$11/yr — Cloudflare Registrar or Porkbun)
12. ☐ Set up website hosting that allows online stores — free options exist (see Hosting)
13. ☐ Set up payment processing account + sales tax calculation; turn on real checkout (see Payments)
14. ☐ Add privacy policy, terms of service, shipping/returns policy, and melt disclaimer pages
15. ☐ Connect email signup form to Klaviyo (free tier)
16. ☐ Set up Pirate Ship for shipping labels (free)
17. ☐ Get a card reader / Tap to Pay for markets; print Certificate of Registration for your booth

**Recurring obligations**
- NC LLC annual report: $200, due April 15 each year
- NC sales tax filing: quarterly (Form E-500) if under ~$100/mo tax due
- Adverse event records: keep 3 years; report serious events to FDA within 15 business days

## 1. Legal & Regulatory

### NC LLC
- Articles of Organization: **$125** one-time, filed online at sosnc.gov (form L-01 on paper). Typical processing 2–3 business days.
- **Annual report: $200/yr, due April 15** (first one the year after formation).
- Registered agent with a physical NC street address is required — you can be your own.

### EIN
Free at irs.gov, issued immediately online. Needed for business banking. The IRS explicitly warns against paid third-party EIN sites.

### NC Sales & Use Tax
- Registration is **free** (NCDOR online portal); account number issued instantly, certificate mailed in ~10 business days.
- Rates: 4.75% state + 2.00–2.25% county (+0.50% transit in a few counties) = **6.75–7.5% combined**, destination-based. Body butter is taxable tangible personal property.
- Filing: NCDOR assigns frequency — **quarterly** if you owe under $100/month (due last day of the month after quarter end), monthly above that (due the 20th). Form E-500, filed online.
- **In-person markets: you must display a copy of your Certificate of Registration at your booth** and give the market operator your registration number — required even for a single event.
- Out-of-state online sales: you only collect other states' tax after crossing economic nexus there (typically **$100K/yr of sales into that state**). Until then, collect NC tax on NC-destined and in-person sales only.

### FDA — MoCRA (federal cosmetics law)
You almost certainly qualify for the **small business exemption**: under **$1M average gross annual US cosmetics sales** (3-year average, inflation-adjusted) exempts you from FDA facility registration, product listing, and the upcoming GMP rule. Body butter and body oil aren't in the excluded categories (eye-area, injected, internal, >24-hr wear). No filing needed to claim it — just keep sales records.

Even exempt, you **must**:
- **Report serious adverse events** to FDA within 15 business days (include a label copy); keep adverse event records 3 years.
- **Maintain safety substantiation** — a file per product showing adequate evidence of safety (existing published data on shea butter, your fragrance suppliers' IFRA certificates and safety data sheets, etc. — no specific test is mandated).
- Meet MoCRA labeling (below).

### Labeling (FDA/FPLA + MoCRA) — applies to every jar and bottle
- **Front panel:** identity ("Whipped Shea Body Butter") + **net weight** in bottom 30%, e.g. "Net Wt 8 oz". 16 oz jar: "Net Wt 16 oz (1 lb)".
- **Ingredient list:** descending order of predominance, INCI names — e.g. *Butyrospermum Parkii (Shea) Butter*. "Fragrance" may be listed as one word for blends. Minimum 1/16" letter height (1/32" if label surface <12 sq in).
- **Business name + address** (street address, city, state, ZIP; "Distributed by" if you don't make it — you do, so plain name/address).
- **MoCRA responsible-person contact** (in effect since Dec 2024): a US address, US phone, **or website** where adverse events can be reported — your domain works.
- No mandatory warning applies to body butter, but without a safety substantiation file FDA could require "Warning—The safety of this product has not been determined." Keep the file.
- **Marketing claims: stay cosmetic.** "Moisturizes," "softens," "glow" = fine. "Heals eczema," "treats stretch marks," "repairs skin" = drug claims = misbranding. Audit site copy too, not just labels.
- **Watch: fragrance allergen labeling.** FDA's proposed rule (per the Unified Agenda) was expected ~May 2026 and had not been issued as of this research. When final, individual fragrance allergens will need to be named on labels — significant for your 13 scents. Recheck quarterly. ([Perkins Coie](https://perkinscoie.com/insights/blog/fdas-unified-regulatory-agenda-key-updates-food-dietary-supplements-and-cosmetics), [NatLawReview](https://natlawreview.com/article/how-mocra-reshaping-fda-oversight-cosmetics-2026))

### NC-specific
- **No state cosmetics manufacturing license exists in NC** (unlike Florida). Home manufacture of cosmetics is legal federally and not restricted by NC statute. *Verified by absence from official license lists — one free confirming call to NCDA&CS Food & Drug Protection (919-733-7366) or EDPNC (1-800-228-8443) makes it airtight.*
- **No general NC business license** exists; municipal privilege license taxes were repealed in 2015.
- Do check: your city/county **home-occupation zoning permit** (production at home usually requires the permitted tier; ~$0–200 one-time depending on municipality) and county **business personal property listing** (equipment) each January.

## 2. Insurance *(optional — required for markets/pop-ups)*

If we sell at markets or pop-ups, this stops being optional: most organizers **require a $1M Certificate of Insurance** naming them as additional insured before you can set up a booth. For online-only selling, no one forces the issue — but product liability still protects against customer claims (e.g., an allergic reaction) no matter where the sale happened, so it's worth considering even without markets. As a cosmetics maker, the coverage that matters is **product liability**, not just general liability. Three credible maker-industry options (all underwritten by the same parent, Veracity; all issue instant COIs with free unlimited additional insureds):

| Option | Annual all-in | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **ACT Pro — Handmade Beauty** | **$515** | No separate dues; fastest to buy online; body butter/body oil explicitly covered. Excludes CBD, paraben/phthalate products. |
| **Indie Business Network** | ~$522–$577 | $247 dues + $275 ($1M) or $330 ($2M) policy |
| **HSCG** | ~$540–$645 | $250 Professional membership + $290 policy ($395 with $30K property coverage for inventory/booth gear) |

Skip per-event policies (~$49–99) — they exclude product liability, which is the coverage that matters for a skincare claim. Homeowners insurance excludes business liability; a cheap rider (~$25/yr) covers home inventory/equipment only.

## 3. Payments

Our website is custom-built, with its own cart and the Sampler's "pick 10" scent selection flow. Some payment options work with what we have; others would replace it with their own store.

### Fee comparison

| Provider | Online fee | In-person fee | Monthly cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stripe | 2.9% + 30¢ | 2.7% + 5¢ (+10¢ if using a phone as the reader) | $0 |
| Square (free plan) | 3.3% + 30¢ | 2.6% + 15¢ | $0 |
| Shopify Basic | 2.9% + 30¢ | 2.6% + 10¢ | $39 ($29 if billed yearly) |
| Shopify Starter | 5% | via their app | $5 |
| Snipcart | ≈ 4.9% + 30¢ all-in | — | $20 minimum if under $1K/mo in sales |
| PayPal buttons | 3.49% + 49¢ | 2.29% + 9¢ | $0 |

### Pros and cons

**Stripe**
- *Pros:* Lowest standard online fee with no monthly cost. Works with our custom site — the cart, scent picker, and sampler flow all stay; customers only leave our design at the final secure payment page. Sends receipt emails automatically. Optional sales tax calculation (Stripe Tax, +0.5% on taxed sales). In-person payments need nothing but a phone.
- *Cons:* Needs a small amount of one-time technical setup to connect to our site (Tré handles this). In-person tools are more bare-bones than Square's — no built-in inventory counts at the booth.

**Square**
- *Pros:* The gold standard for in-person selling — polished register app, $59 reader or phone-only, and inventory stays in sync between online and in-person sales automatically. Free plan that includes a free basic online store.
- *Cons:* Highest standard online rate (3.3% + 30¢). Its online store is Square's own builder — using it for checkout means giving up our custom-designed site, or running two sites.

**Shopify Basic**
- *Pros:* The most complete all-in-one: store, inventory, discounted shipping labels, and in-person selling in one place. Competitive fees.
- *Cons:* $39/month whether or not we sell anything. We'd either rebuild the site on their platform or bolt their buy buttons onto ours — both compromise the custom design.

**Shopify Starter**
- *Pros:* Cheap ($5/mo) and quick; good for selling through links and social media.
- *Cons:* 5% per sale — the highest fee here. No real website included.

**Snipcart**
- *Pros:* Built specifically to add a cart to custom websites like ours with little setup.
- *Cons:* Effectively ~4.9% + 30¢ once fees stack, plus a $20/mo minimum at low sales volume. The company shows little sign of active development — a risk for something this central.

**PayPal buttons**
- *Pros:* Zero setup; customers know and trust it. Worth offering *alongside* a main processor, since some shoppers prefer paying with PayPal.
- *Cons:* Higher fees (3.49% + 49¢), and buttons sell one item at a time — no real cart, so it can't be the primary checkout.

### Recommendations

**Online only: Stripe.** Lowest cost, no monthly fee, keeps our custom site and sampler flow intact, and handles receipts and NC sales tax math automatically. Nothing else matches that combination.

**Online + markets/pop-ups: still Stripe, for both.** Using Stripe's phone-based tap-to-pay (or its $59 card reader) at the booth keeps every sale — web and in-person — in one dashboard with one set of reports, which makes bookkeeping and sales tax filing much simpler. The alternative worth knowing: if markets ever become the *main* sales channel and booth inventory tracking matters more than the website, **Square** is the better in-person system (lower in-person fee, best-in-class register app) — the tradeoff is a second platform to reconcile and a pricier online rate.

## 4. Shipping

### Rates (from NC; most of the East Coast is zones 2–4)
- **Default: USPS Ground Advantage via Pirate Ship** (free platform, pass-through commercial rates, no markup). Realistic cost ~**$4.50–$8.50** for a 1–2 lb package depending on zone; 2–5 days; $100 insurance + tracking included — covers every order at your price points.
- Priority Mail (1–3 days) for summer or rush; **Priority Mail Cubic in your own branded box usually beats Medium/Large Flat Rate** for the Sampler.
- Note: USPS has a **temporary 8% surcharge April 26, 2026 – Jan 17, 2027** — bake it into your shipping prices now.
- UPS/FedEx aren't competitive at 1–3 lb; revisit only for heavy wholesale boxes.

### The melt problem (this is your #1 operational policy)
Whipped shea softens at ~76°F and collapses when melted. Industry-standard handling:
- **Publish a melt disclaimer**: melting in transit is a natural reaction to heat, doesn't affect quality, and is **not eligible for refund** — texture/volume change only. Pair it with education copy (refrigerate before opening; re-whip with a hand mixer). Wild Seed Botanicals' summer-shipping page is a good model for an on-brand FAQ.
- **Ship Monday–Tuesday only** in warm months so packages never sit in a hot facility over a weekend.
- Summer options: upgrade to Priority, thermal bubble mailers (~$0.50–1.50/unit), gel packs (~$0.50–2/unit but add billable weight), or a small optional "summer shipping" fee (~$3–8 — varies by shop). Some sellers pause butter sales June–Sept; with 13 scents and an oil, you can keep the Silk Body Oil and sampler oils year-round.
- Carrier insurance covers loss/crush, **never melting** — the disclaimer is your risk management.

### Packaging & returns
- Jars need **boxes + bubble wrap** (mailers crush lids); the 10 ml oil can ship in a padded mailer. Tape or shrink-band lids. No dimensional-weight worries at your sizes — USPS bills actual weight under 1 cubic foot.
- **Returns policy norm for cosmetics: all sales final** (hygiene — opened product can't be resold). Damaged/wrong-item claims within 48–72 hours with photos, resolved by replacement or store credit. Melted ≠ damaged. **Display the policy conspicuously at checkout** — some states require pre-purchase disclosure of no-return policies.

## 5. Hosting & Site Infrastructure

### Website hosting
Important: not every hosting service's free plan allows running a store — some prohibit commercial use in their fine print. Two well-regarded free options that **explicitly allow online stores**:

- **Cloudflare Pages** — free with no traffic fees, includes the security certificate (the padlock in the browser) and supports the behind-the-scenes piece our checkout needs. Strongest free option; **recommended**.
- **Netlify (free plan)** — also allows commercial use and supports everything we need; has monthly usage limits worth confirming at signup.

Tré handles the technical setup either way; the monthly cost is $0.

### Domain
Our web address: ~$10.50–11/yr at Cloudflare Registrar or Porkbun (.com prices rise ~7% in Nov 2026). The security certificate is free from every host — never pay separately for one.

### Legal pages (required before launch)
- California's privacy law (CCPA) doesn't apply to a business our size ($26M+ revenue or 100K+ CA consumers), and NC has no comprehensive privacy law — **but Stripe's merchant agreement and Google Analytics' terms contractually require a posted privacy policy**. Klaviyo's terms too.
- Minimum set: **Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Shipping & Returns (incl. melt policy)**. Termly generates one free privacy policy + one free T&C.
- Cookie banner: not legally required for a US-only small site using plain analytics. Skip it.

### Email
- **Order confirmations: Stripe sends receipts automatically** — nothing to build at launch. Custom-branded order emails can be added later at no cost (Resend, free up to 3,000/mo).
- **The site's signup form → Klaviyo free tier** (250 profiles, 500 sends/mo; better e-commerce fit than Mailchimp at identical free limits).
- **CAN-SPAM** applies to every marketing email: physical postal address in the footer (a PO box works — keeps your home address private, and can double as your label address), working unsubscribe honored within 10 business days. Klaviyo automates the mechanics. Penalties run to ~$53K per email, so don't freelance this.

## 7. Open Items / Verify Before Relying

1. **MoCRA inflation-adjusted threshold** — FDA hasn't published an official adjusted figure; plan around $1M.
2. **Fragrance allergen rule** — proposed rule was due ~May 2026; check fda.gov/cosmetics now and quarterly.
3. **NC "no cosmetics license"** — confirmed by absence from official lists; one call to NCDA&CS (919-733-7366) closes it.
4. **Your city's home-occupation permit** — rules and costs are municipal; check your planning department.
